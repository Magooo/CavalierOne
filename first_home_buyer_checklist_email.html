What Does It All Mean?

What is LVR (Loan to Value Ratio)?

LVR is the percentage of your loan compared to the value of the property. For example, if your land and home are valued at $400,000 and you borrow $360,000, your LVR is 90%. The lower your LVR, the less risk you represent to a lender — which often means better interest rates and fewer conditions on your loan.

How much deposit do you typically need?

Most lenders require a minimum of 5% of the property value as a deposit, though 10–20% is more comfortable:

  • 5% deposit — Get into the market sooner, but you'll likely pay Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI).
  • 10% deposit — A stronger position. Some lenders will waive LMI.
  • 20% deposit — No LMI and typically better loan terms. Worth reaching if possible.
Why speak to a mortgage broker early?

A good mortgage broker gives you a realistic picture of your borrowing power before you fall in love with a floor plan or block of land. They compare dozens of lenders on your behalf and help you avoid nasty surprises. It's one of the best first calls you can make.

The risks of overextending financially

It can be tempting to stretch your budget for extras. A comfortable buffer — typically 10% of your total build budget — can be the difference between a stressful build and an enjoyable one.

Key Information

Victorian Government Incentives for First Home Buyers

Important: Government grants and schemes change regularly. Always verify current eligibility at sro.vic.gov.au and nhfic.gov.au or speak to your mortgage broker.

Incentives Checklist

  • Check your eligibility for the First Home Owner Grant (FHOG)
  • Find out if you qualify for stamp duty exemption or concession
  • Ask your broker about the Victorian Homebuyer Fund (shared equity)
  • Explore the Federal First Home Guarantee — 5% deposit, no LMI
  • Look into the Regional First Home Buyer Guarantee for Goulburn Valley buyers
  • Ask about the Family Home Guarantee if you are a single parent
Victorian Government
1. First Home Owner Grant (FHOG) — $10,000

A one-off $10,000 grant for eligible first home buyers building or purchasing a brand new home valued at $750,000 or less. Paid when construction commences. You must be an Australian citizen or permanent resident, never previously owned property in Australia, and intend to live in the home for at least 12 months.

Victorian Government
2. Stamp Duty Exemption & Concession

First home buyers can save significantly on land transfer duty:

  • Full exemption — Properties valued at $600,000 or less → $0 stamp duty
  • Sliding scale concession — Properties valued $600,001–$750,000
  • For new builds, duty is typically calculated on the land value only — meaning many Goulburn Valley buyers pay little to no stamp duty at all
Victorian Government
3. Victorian Homebuyer Fund (Shared Equity)

The government contributes up to 25% of the purchase price in exchange for a share in the property. You only need a 5% deposit, avoid LMI, and borrow less. Property cap: $600,000 in regional Victoria. Income limits apply. You can buy back the government's share over time as your finances grow.

Federal Government
4. First Home Guarantee — 5% Deposit, No LMI

Purchase with just a 5% deposit without paying Lenders Mortgage Insurance. The government guarantees up to 15% of the loan. Limited places available each financial year through participating lenders — your mortgage broker will know which ones.

Federal Government — Regional
5. Regional First Home Buyer Guarantee

Specifically designed for buyers in regional areas like the Goulburn Valley and Shepparton. Same benefits as the First Home Guarantee (5% deposit, no LMI) but with reserved places for regional buyers. Less competition than the metro version — perfect if you're building in Shepparton, Kyabram, Echuca, or surrounding areas.

Federal Government
6. Family Home Guarantee

For single parents and legal guardians. Purchase with as little as a 2% deposit with government guarantee — no LMI payable.

How these incentives stack up: Many Goulburn Valley first home buyers can combine the FHOG ($10,000) + stamp duty exemption + Regional First Home Buyer Guarantee to save $20,000–$40,000 compared to buying without any assistance. Speak to a mortgage broker to find out exactly what applies to your situation.

Section 2

Renting While Saving: How Your Rent Can Work For You

  • Ask your mortgage broker if your lender accepts rent as genuine savings
  • Ensure your rent is paid via bank transfer — not cash
  • Maintain consistent, on-time rental payments for at least 3–6 months
  • Obtain a rental ledger from your property manager as evidence
  • Understand what other forms of genuine savings your lender accepts
  • Don't assume all lenders treat rental history the same way
What is "genuine savings"?

When borrowing with less than a 20% deposit, most lenders want to see genuine savings — money set aside over time that demonstrates financial discipline. This is where being a renter can actually work in your favour.

How rental payments can count as genuine savings

Some lenders accept a 12-month history of consistent rental payments as evidence that you can manage a regular financial commitment — exactly what a mortgage requires. Not every bank offers this, which is exactly why a mortgage broker is so valuable.

How to make your rental history count
  • At least 3–6 months of consistent, on-time payments (some lenders require 12 months)
  • Payments made via direct bank transfer — not cash (cash leaves no paper trail)
  • A rental ledger from your property manager showing your full payment history
  • Rental payments must be in your own name
Section 3

Buying Land

  • Decide between a house & land package vs. buying land separately
  • Check if the land title has been registered
  • Understand the soil classification of the block
  • Review any estate design guidelines
  • Get a clear picture of expected site costs
House and land packages vs. buying land separately

A house and land package bundles both — a convenient, streamlined option for first home buyers. Buying land separately gives you more choice in builder and design, but requires more coordination. Both approaches have merit.

What is a soil classification?

Australian land is classified by soil type, affecting how your slab and footings are engineered — from Class A (stable sandy soil) to Class P (problem sites). The higher the classification, the more engineering required — and the higher the site costs. Your builder should conduct a soil test during pre-construction and provide an updated site cost estimate before you commit.

Why site costs vary between blocks

Two blocks side by side can have very different site costs depending on soil classification, slope, existing vegetation, proximity to services, and any retaining walls required. Always ask for an estimated site cost range before exchanging contracts.

Section 4

Construction Types

  • Understand the difference between timber frame and steel frame construction
  • Consider energy efficiency in your framing choice
  • Think about long-term structural strength
  • Consider future renovation or modification needs
Timber frame construction

The most common method in Australian residential construction — well understood by tradespeople, widely available, and cost-effective. Timber is also easier to work with for future renovations.

Steel frame construction

Termite-resistant and dimensionally stable, but can be more expensive upfront and less familiar to local trades. Steel also conducts heat and cold more readily, which may affect thermal comfort if insulation isn't carefully specified.

Why timber is often preferred in the Goulburn Valley

For most first home buyers, timber framing is the practical, affordable, and flexible choice — tried, tested, and well-suited to regional Victoria's conditions. Cavalier Homes uses T2 termite-treated timber frame and trusses as standard.

Section 5

Choosing the Right Builder

  • Choose a builder with strong local experience
  • Read reviews and ask for references
  • Understand exactly what's included in your contract price
  • Confirm they offer fixed price contracts
  • Ask about communication during the build
  • Watch for red flags — vague contracts, pressure tactics, or unusually low quotes
Red flags to watch for
  • Vague contracts with large contingency clauses
  • Pressure to sign before you've had time to consider
  • Unusually low quotes that seem too good to be true
  • Limited local presence or online reviews
  • Reluctance to provide references
Section 6

Designing Your First Home

  • Choose a floor plan that suits your actual lifestyle
  • Think about future family needs
  • Prioritise storage and practical functionality
  • Consider your home's orientation for natural light
  • Build in energy efficiency from the start
Orientation and natural light

In the Goulburn Valley, north-facing living areas capture winter sun and significantly reduce heating costs year-round. Discuss orientation with your builder during the design stage — it costs nothing to get right and saves money for decades.

Storage and functionality

Never underestimate storage. Linen cupboards, walk-in pantries, and smart bedroom storage make an enormous difference to everyday life. Ask your builder about maximising storage in your chosen design.

Section 7

Avoiding Common First Home Buyer Mistakes

  • Don't spend your entire budget on upgrades and extras
  • Don't underestimate site costs — always get a written estimate
  • Don't choose the cheapest builder without thorough research
  • Don't forget to plan for real lifestyle needs
  • Don't skip getting independent financial and legal advice
Spending all your budget on upgrades

Stone benchtops and upgraded kitchens are wonderful — but they add up fast during selections. Set a firm upgrade budget and stick to it. You can always renovate later, once you're settled in.

Underestimating site costs

Site costs can range from a few thousand dollars on a flat, stable block to tens of thousands on a sloped or reactive soil site. Never assume site costs are included in an advertised price — always ask for a written estimate based on an actual soil test.

Choosing the cheapest builder

The cheapest quote rarely represents the best value. Research thoroughly, compare contracts carefully, and choose based on value — not just price.
